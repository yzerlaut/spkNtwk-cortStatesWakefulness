* Abstract

Despite the large body of experimental work highlighting the critical role of state-dependent computations in the awake cortex, a theoretical framework describing such phenomenon is still lacking. The present study builds a minimal spiking model of a local cortical assembly and examines its dynamical and computational properties. It is first shown that a weak intrinsic rhythmicity coupled with competing inhibitory and disinhibitory circuits make the network generates the set of dynamical regimes known as the “U-model” of cortical states: the regimes observed at various arousal levels in rodent sensory cortices. We then test the functional specificity of such network states in two vision-inspired tasks. In a natural scene discrimination task, the network encoding is found to be optimal when displaying the sparse activity regime associated to intermediate arousal levels. In a light detection task, the network is optimally detecting when displaying the dense activity regimes found at high arousal levels. Finally, the model proposes a functional role for the rhythmic activity characterizing the idling cortex, it insures unambiguous transitions between the function-specific sparse and dense desynchronized modes of cortical activity. The proposed model might provide a foundational building block to understand the functional flexibility mediated by spike-based dynamics in neocortex during wakefulness.
